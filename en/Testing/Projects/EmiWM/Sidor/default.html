Report on emissions from machinery

SMP reported in early 2007, a study commissioned Swedish Environmental Protection Agency. The mission was carried out in collaboration with the Department of Biometry and Engineering, SLU and the Swedish Environmental Research Institute.

The study surveyed both the current situation regarding emissions from large diesel-driven machinery in the power range from 37 to 560 kW, and made ​​projections of expected emissions in 2010 and 2020. ​

Click here to download the report "Emissions from large diesel-driven machinery".

The report provides an updated picture of emissions

The study covered machines such as tractors, loaders, excavators, dump trucks cranes, harvesters, forestry and trucks.

The report provides an updated and compared to previous reports, altered picture of emissions from heavy machinery. One example is the noxious emissions of particulate matter, which is expected to be substantially lower than previously assumed.

The mapping of emissions based on data where machine types, the number of machines, motor, operating times, load factors, fuel consumption, emission factors and how these parameters are related to machine age where machine age is specified for up to 25 years old machines while older machines are managed as a group.

Data pertaining to machine types, number of machines, machine age and engine power as well as annual operating times were taken from SMP Swedish Machinery Testing testing database, from sales from equipment suppliers and from the vehicle register. These data are considered to be very safe for the majority of the studied types of machines.

New concepts on the study

When it comes to fuel consumption and emission factors used data from CORINAIR Emission Inventory Guidebook and the European emission legislation as a base. These factors have been adjusted to take account of

  • Differences between the fuel used for type testing and Swedish MK1
  • Differences between legal values ​​and measured emission levels in type testing
  • Differences between the engine load in static test cycles and real engine load
  • Age contingent changes in fuel consumption and emission values

    • These corrections as well as taking into account the machine age, which is of great importance because the running time is highly dependent on the machine age, makes the design of the study distinguish it from several previously published Swedish emission inventories of similar nature.

    290 000 active machines

    The study assumed a total of 290,000 machines to be active in 2006, which is 25% more when compared to the most recently published similar study (Flodström et al., 2004).

    The updated total fuel consumption and total amount of emissions from large diesel-driven machinery for 2006 are summarized below.

    Number of machines: 290 000 Fuel consumption: 880 000 tonnes/year CO2: 2 800 000 CO: 6 000 tonnes/year HC: 2 200 tonnes/year NOx: 23 000 tonnes/year PM: 1 000 tonnes/year SOx: 1,8 tonnes/year

    The assessment of total annual consumption is approximately 5% lower than in the previous study, despite the higher number of machines. For emissions, the differences are even greater when compared with the above study: levels of NOx is halved while both CO and HC corresponds to about 1/3 of the previously estimated levels.

    The main difference is found, however, for particles for which the estimated emissions have been reduced by about 75%. These differences are expected to be mainly due to improved data, particularly when it comes to operating times related to machine age and the corrections made in the model, rather than actual changes in equipment stocks, etc.
